> Working with artist IC Jaucian, I want to make an interactive, robotics-based artwork where we simulate swarming behavior, and which people can interact with. It will build on a previous robotic artwork that he had previously done, involving floor-bound automated robots interacting with audience members in a gallery space.   On its own, the artistic outputs (especially if they get exhibited more widely) and documentation (and analysis) of the creative research process are already contributions. What we are hoping is that in our review of related works and related literature, we won’t find a lot of actual robotic swarms in an artistic context in which the each individual robot is actually behaving like an autonomous agent with its own simple set of rules and reacting only to local conditions. For example, in the “Murmuring Minds” artwork (https://studiodrift.com/work/murmuring-minds/) we strongly suspect that the work uses computer vision to detect the location of an audience member on the floor, and then a centralized computer computes where each of the moving blocks should be placed. The computation, in other words, is still centralized, not distributed, which is what a true swarm-based system would be like. 
> 
> But then, on top of that, I want to study the artistic work as a kind of educational intervention, and investigate whether this kind of creative, tangible, interactive, play-based system can create a measurably different learning experience involving teaching complex systems, swarm behavior, etc., in the local context. So for example, an educator (who will keep in the back of their minds the pedagogical value of the artwork) and a mathematical biologist will be involved  in multiple steps of the creative process, advising and providing suggestions to the creative team—science informing art. But then when the artwork is done, we can run some studies on it, for example, to see whether individuals can have a deeper, or more memorable, engagement with the concept of complex systems, either in a gallery setting or in a controlled classroom setting. Maybe we can do a randomized control study, but we would be open to other kinds of ways of studying this. Perhaps we could even do a very long-term follow-up study, where people who experienced the artwork could be contacted, say, five years from now to see whether that experience was memorable, or maybe changed the way they thought about the subject, or even influenced their interest and career path. 
> 
> The structure of the research would be very much based on my Haplos doctoral thesis project, wherein I created an artistic technology (which in and of itself was an interesting artistic device embodying a particular philosophy about the body, i.e., Schusterman’s somaesthetics), which created novel aesthetic vibrotactile experiences for its wearers, but also served a functional purpose (increasing body awareness). And then it turned out that the device had some other functional purpose (i.e., decreasing food cravings in a lab settings), which was tested in a separate study.

> ## Research Questions
> 
> **Creative Question:** How can a swarm of simple autonomous robots be designed as an interactive art installation to _embody_ decentralized, emergent swarming behavior? How do variations in local stimuli (e.g. proximity, light, sound) change the collective behavior? > **Educational/Empirical Question:** How does engaging with an embodied swarm installation support complex systems thinking in learners? Sub-questions include: Does participation in the swarm artwork increase learners’ understanding of emergent, non-linear system behavior and collective phenomena? How do play-based interactions with the swarm affect learners’ ability to take different system “perspectives” (e.g. an agent vs. a whole swarm) in reasoning about complex processes? How do factors like collaboration among learners or multi-sensory elements influence engagement and learning outcomes?

> ## Purpose

> **General Aim:** The project seeks to create a novel _Swarm Artwork_ – an interactive installation where many simple robots self-organize into dynamic, emergent behavior – and to investigate its potential as a STEAM-based learning tool. The installation will incorporate visual and auditory elements (lights, projections, sounds) to translate the robots’ internal state and interactions into an aesthetic experience.
